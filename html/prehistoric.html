Prehistoric Life

Prehistoric life represents the earliest chapters of Earth‚Äôs biological history, spanning billions of years before written records. It reveals how life originated, evolved, and adapted in a constantly changing planet.

Origins of Life on Earth

Life on Earth is believed to have begun over 3.5 billion years ago in the form of simple microscopic organisms. These early life forms emerged in Earth‚Äôs primordial oceans under extreme environmental conditions.

Through chemical evolution, simple organic molecules formed complex structures capable of growth, reproduction, and adaptation ‚Äî laying the foundation for biological evolution.

Geological Time Scale

Prehistoric life is studied through the geological time scale, which divides Earth‚Äôs history into eons, eras, periods, and epochs. Each division reflects significant biological and geological changes.

Major eras such as the Paleozoic, Mesozoic, and Cenozoic witnessed the rise and fall of diverse life forms, including marine organisms, reptiles, mammals, and early humans.

Key Insight

More than 99% of all species that ever lived on Earth are now extinct, making fossils essential for understanding prehistoric biodiversity.

Age of Dinosaurs

The Mesozoic Era, often called the ‚ÄúAge of Dinosaurs,‚Äù was dominated by large reptiles that evolved into a wide range of forms adapted to land, sea, and air.

Dinosaurs coexisted with early mammals, plants, and insects, forming complex ecosystems long before human existence.

Mass Extinctions

Earth has experienced multiple mass extinction events that drastically reshaped life. These events were caused by volcanic activity, climate change, asteroid impacts, and oceanic disturbances.

The extinction of dinosaurs approximately 66 million years ago allowed mammals to diversify and eventually dominate terrestrial ecosystems.

Rise of Early Humans

Prehistoric life also includes early human ancestors who evolved over millions of years. Fossil evidence shows gradual changes in brain size, posture, tool use, and social behavior.

These developments marked the transition from biological evolution to cultural and technological advancement.

Why Prehistoric Life Matters

Studying prehistoric life helps scientists understand climate change, biodiversity loss, and evolutionary resilience ‚Äî offering valuable insights for protecting life on Earth today.
